My 3D Solar System Simulation — How It Actually Happened
I started with a simple question: what if instead of faking planetary orbits with pre-drawn circles, I let real gravitational physics drive the motion? That question turned into a week-long project that took me from Google Colab all the way to a GPU-powered 3D simulation running on my own machine.
The first version was 2D, built in Python with NumPy and Matplotlib. And honestly, the first few attempts were a mess. The first thing that tripped me up was velocity — I couldn't understand why each planet needed a sideways speed at all. Turns out without it, there's nothing fighting gravity, and every planet just falls straight into the Sun. Once that clicked, orbital mechanics started making intuitive sense.
The next thing that broke was acceleration. My planets kept flying off the screen, and it took me a while to figure out why. I was stacking accelerations — never resetting them to zero each timestep. Gravity from the previous frame was adding on top of the current frame's gravity, and it snowballed until the forces were completely unrealistic. The fix was simple once I understood it: erase the whiteboard every step, then recalculate from scratch.
Then came the scaling problem. When I finally got the planets moving, Mercury was basically sitting inside the Sun. The issue was that Saturn is 24 times further from the Sun than Mercury — when you fit that on one screen, the inner planets get crushed into a tiny cluster near the center. I had to think carefully about how to represent those distances visually without losing the inner solar system entirely.
Colab served me well for learning, but it had a ceiling. The animation flickered constantly because every frame required deleting and redrawing the entire figure from scratch. I tried building a zoom slider, but it couldn't interact with pre-rendered animations. I wanted something smoother — real-time, interactive, 60fps. That meant leaving Colab behind and setting up a proper local environment in VS Code with Vispy, a GPU-accelerated rendering library.
The transition wasn't seamless. The first error I hit was Vispy refusing to open a window at all — it turns out it needs a separate backend just to create a window on screen. Installing PyQt6 fixed that. Then the window started opening and immediately closing, which was confusing until I realized the program was simply finishing execution and exiting. Adding app.run() at the end kept the event loop alive and the window open.
The final result was something I'm genuinely proud of. Eight bodies simultaneously pulling on each other with real gravitational forces, rendered in 3D with orbital trails, a background of 2,000 stars, and a camera I can rotate, zoom, and pan freely with my mouse. The physics engine is the same N-body system I built in Colab — just extended with a Z-axis and handed off to the GPU to render.
Going from not knowing what vx meant to watching planets orbit in real-time 3D physics is a pretty good feeling.
